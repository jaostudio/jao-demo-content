## 1. Landing Demo

**URL:** `https://jao-demo-landing.vercel.app/`

### Current Messaging

- Homepage hero: "Conversion-Optimized Landing System"
- Tagline variant: "Lead Generation Platform"
- Sub-copy focuses on vertical-specific pages, capture forms, SEO content

### 5-Second Test

**Fail.** A dentist or plumber sees "Conversion-Optimized Landing System" and has no idea what that means. "Lead Generation Platform" is marginally better but still jargon-y. A non-technical business owner probably leaves within 3 seconds.

### Problems with Current Approach

- The word "system" is developer-speak for something that is not a system to the buyer — it's just a website that gets them customers.
- "Conversion-Optimized" tries to sound data-driven but adds noise. The buyer cares about _results_, not optimization mechanics.
- No social proof or outcome anchoring above the fold (e.g., "X appointments booked this month").

### Weaknesses

- Loses the vertical-specific strength — the sub-pages (dentist, contractor, real estate) are the real differentiator, but the homepage doesn't lead with them.
- No clear "who this is for" signal. Buyer has to self-identify through reading, not scanning.
- CTA likely generic ("Get Started") instead of outcome-driven ("Start Getting Leads").

### Reference Products & Positioning Patterns

| Product  | Pattern                                          |
| -------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| HubSpot  | "Grow better" — outcome only, zero tech          |
| Thumbtack| "Get matched with top pros" — buyer-language     |
| Squarespace | "Create a website for your next big idea" — aspirational, not mechanical |

### What We Can Borrow

- **HubSpot's outcome-only headline structure.** "Turn visitors into appointments" beats "Conversion-Optimized Landing System."
- **Thumbtack's audience-first framing.** Show the three verticals visually (dentist, contractor, realtor) immediately.
- **Squarespace's aspirational tone.** Small business owners want to feel like they're leveling up, not buying a tool.

### What We Should Avoid

- "Conversion-Optimized" — prefab SaaS language that has been optimized for SEO, not for humans.
- "Landing System" — never use "system" to describe what a non-technical buyer buys.
- Listing features (forms, CRM sync, SEO) before naming the outcome.

## 2. Commerce Demo

**URL:** `https://jao-demo-commerce.vercel.app/`

### Current Messaging

- "Full e-commerce lifecycle: product catalog, cart, checkout, payments, and order management"
- Positioning per [`positioning.md`](#) : Revenue Operations Platform

### 5-Second Test

**Fail.** Reads like a developer README, not a homepage. A business owner scanning for "can this grow my revenue?" sees a bullet list of features and gets no answer.

### Problems with Current Approach

- Zero business outcome in the headline. The entire hero is a feature inventory.
- "Revenue Operations Platform" is not in the current copy — the homepage doesn't even use the positioning language.
- A prospect comparing against Shopify or BigCommerce has no reason to pick this one. What's the unfair advantage?

### Weaknesses

- No mention of the core positioning problem: businesses that have outgrown spreadsheets and disconnected tools.
- No social proof, no scale indicators ("trusted by X merchants").
- "Full e-commerce lifecycle" could describe any e-commerce platform. Zero differentiation.

### Reference Products & Positioning Patterns

| Product | Pattern |
| ------- | ------- |
| Shopify | "Your business, your brand, your way" — emotional, not technical |
| Stripe  | "Financial infrastructure for the internet" — ambitious framing |
| Square  | "Start taking payments" — action-oriented, low friction |

### What We Can Borrow

- **Stripe's infrastructure framing** but focused on operations: "Revenue operations for businesses that outgrew spreadsheets."
- **Shopify's emotional hook** — the buyer wants to feel in control of their business, not like they're buying a piece of software.
- **Square's action orientation** — lead with "From catalog to delivery" (copy from positioning doc) instead of listing components.

### What We Should Avoid

- The feature list headline. Restructure entirely around outcome: "One platform for your entire revenue operation."
- "Lifecycle" — technical abstraction. Use process language a merchant uses: "list, sell, ship, track."

## 3. Marketplace Demo

**URL:** `https://jao-demo-marketplace.vercel.app/`

### Current Messaging

- Hero: "Discover Unique Goods"
- Header: "Multi-Vendor Platform"
- Positioning per [`positioning.md`](#) : Multi-Vendor Commerce Platform

### 5-Second Test

**Mixed.** "Discover Unique Goods" sounds like a consumer tagline, not a B2B platform pitch. A business owner building a marketplace would scan for something about _running_ a marketplace, not _shopping_ on one. "Multi-Vendor Platform" in the header is where they might stop — but it's generic.

### Problems with Current Approach

- The hero speaks to the _end customer_, not the _platform owner_. The buyer (a marketplace operator) is not addressed.
- "Multi-Vendor Platform" is the tech category, not a value proposition.
- No mention of vendor management, commissions, or dispute resolution — the actual pain points.

### Weaknesses

- The homepage is treating the demo as if it's the marketplace itself, not a demonstration of marketplace _software_.
- No audience signal. Who is this for? Curated marketplace owners? Enterprise procurement platforms?
- The positioning doc says "Built for curated marketplaces, not garage sales" — this is strong differentiation that doesn't appear.

### Reference Products & Positioning Patterns

| Product | Pattern |
| ------- | ------- |
| Etsy    | "We keep commerce human" — values-driven, emotional |
| Amazon  | "We strive to offer our customers the lowest possible prices" — customer-obsessed |
| Sharetribe | "Launch your marketplace in days" — speed/outcome for platform owners |

### What We Can Borrow

- **Sharetribe's platform-owner-first framing.** The buyer is the person launching the marketplace, not shopping on it.
- **Etsy's humanity** — "curated" and "independent vendors" are emotional differentiators worth leading with.
- **Clear audience hook** from positioning doc: "Run a marketplace, not a spreadsheet of vendors."

### What We Should Avoid

- "Discover Unique Goods" — this is a CTA for a shopper, not a value prop for a platform buyer.
- Consumer-facing copy on a B2B demo. Keep the primary audience (the operator) in every sentence.

## 4. Content Demo

**URL:** `https://jao-demo-content.vercel.app/`

### Current Messaging

- "A publishing platform with editorial workflows and SEO optimization"
- Positioning per [`positioning.md`](#) : Editorial Workflow Platform

### 5-Second Test

**Passable but weak.** An editorial director or content operations manager would recognize "editorial workflows." But "publishing platform" is generic (WordPress is a publishing platform). The SEO mention is a bonus but not a differentiator.

### Problems with Current Approach

- "Publishing platform" is underselling — the differentiator is the _workflow governance_ (state machine from draft to publish), not the publishing itself.
- The copy is descriptive, not aspirational. It describes what it is, not what it solves.
- Loses the team/pain-point angle: "no more 'where's that article?' Slack messages" (from positioning doc) is a human hook that isn't used.

### Weaknesses

- No hero mention of editorial teams, approval bottlenecks, or pipeline visibility.
- "SEO optimization" is table stakes, not a headline feature.
- The positioning doc's problem statement (articles stuck in email drafts) is much stronger than what the homepage communicates.

### Reference Products & Positioning Patterns

| Product   | Pattern |
| --------- | ------- |
| WordPress | "Create your website with limitless possibilities" — expansive but generic |
| Medium    | "Where words matter" — writer-centric, minimalist |
| Substack  | "A new economic engine for culture" — ambitious, writer-empowerment |

### What We Can Borrow

- **Medium's writer-centric tone** but focused on the editor/manager: "From pitch to publish, tracked at every state."
- **Substack's ambitious framing** — position this as how professional editorial teams operate at scale.
- The pain-point hook from the positioning doc: "No more 'where's that article?' Slack messages."

### What We Should Avoid

- "Publishing platform" without qualification. Every CMS is a publishing platform.
- Feature-listing (SEO, categories, roles) before the outcome. Lead with workflow visibility.

## 5. WebApp Demo

**URL:** `https://jao-demo-webapp.vercel.app/`

### Current Messaging

- "Multi-tenant, Kanban, task machine, activity log, org-level settings"
- Feature-dump in the hero section
- Positioning per [`positioning.md`](#) : Internal Operations Platform

### 5-Second Test

**Fail.** A VP of Operations or COO sees a wall of technology nouns and has no takeaway. Kanban? Task machine? These are components, not outcomes. The page does not answer "what does this do for my company?"

### Problems with Current Approach

- The hero violates **positioning rule #1** (never lead with technology) and **rule #2** (always lead with outcome).
- No clear single audience. "Multi-tenant" suggests enterprise IT, but "Kanban" suggests product teams. Mixed signals.
- The positioning doc targets operations teams managing multiple business units — the homepage doesn't reflect this at all.

### Weaknesses

- Worst offender of the six demos on tech-first messaging.
- No mention of cross-org visibility, team isolation, or unified operations — the actual value prop.
- "Task machine" is internal jargon that means nothing to a buyer.

### Reference Products & Positioning Patterns

| Product | Pattern |
| ------- | ------- |
| Linear  | "Linear is a purpose-built tool for planning and building products" — clear audience, clear job |
| Notion  | "Your connected workspace for docs, projects, and wikis" — all-in-one, but with a unifying concept |
| Asana   | "Manage your team's work, projects, and tasks" — action-oriented, team-first |

### What We Can Borrow

- **Linear's clarity of audience.** State who this is for in the headline.
- **Notion's unifying concept** — "one platform for every org in your company" (from positioning doc) is the unifying idea.
- **Asana's action verb framing** — "Manage," "Track," "Unify" are stronger than "Multi-tenant" or "Kanban."

### What We Should Avoid

- "Multi-tenant" — never in client-facing copy. This is an architecture detail.
- "Task machine" — eliminate entirely.
- Feature-dump hero. Restructure around: audience, problem, outcome.

## 6. Security Demo

**URL:** `https://jao-demo-security.vercel.app/`

### Current Messaging

- "Secure client portal demonstrating auditability, authorization, rate limiting, and real-time event streaming"
- Positioning per [`positioning.md`](#) : Compliance & Audit Platform

### 5-Second Test

**Fail.** A compliance officer reads "auditability, authorization, rate limiting, real-time event streaming" and checks out. Too technical, too dense, no immediate relevance to their job (passing an audit).

### Problems with Current Approach

- Leads with implementation details (rate limiting, event streaming) that only engineers care about.
- The compliance/audit outcome (the actual buyer need) is buried or absent.
- "Secure client portal" sounds like a login page, not a compliance solution.

### Weaknesses

- The copy speaks to a security engineer, but the buyer is a compliance officer or IT administrator (per positioning doc).
- No mention of SOC 2, HIPAA, or audit readiness — the exact triggers that make someone search for this.
- "Real-time event streaming" is impressive tech but meaningless to an auditor.

### Reference Products & Positioning Patterns

| Product | Pattern |
| ------- | ------- |
| Vanta   | "Automate your SOC 2 compliance" — outcome-first, compliance-specific |
| Drata   | "Achieve and maintain compliance effortlessly" — low-friction, aspirational |
| Sentry  | "See errors clearly" — simple, outcome-focused (even for a dev tool) |

### What We Can Borrow

- **Vanta's compliance-first framing.** Lead with the regulation or outcome: "Built for SOC 2, HIPAA, and internal compliance teams."
- **Drata's "effortless" angle.** Compliance buyers are stressed — promise ease.
- **Sentry's simplicity.** "Who did what, when — and who allowed it" (from positioning doc) is a perfect one-liner.

### What We Should Avoid

- "Auditability" — an abstract noun that sounds like a feature checkbox. Use "audit trail" or "full visibility."
- "Rate limiting" and "event streaming" in the headline. These belong in a technical appendix.
- "Secure client portal" — too generic. Replace with "Compliance Dashboard" or "Audit-Ready Platform."

## Cross-Demo Patterns & Recommendations

### Common Homepage Problems

| Issue | Affected Demos |
| ----- | -------------- |
| Feature-list hero (no outcome) | Commerce, WebApp, Security |
| Tech jargon in headline | Landing ("system"), WebApp ("multi-tenant"), Security ("rate limiting") |
| Wrong audience addressed | Marketplace (speaks to shopper, not operator) |
| Positioning not reflected in copy | All 6 — none fully align with `positioning.md` language yet |

### Quick Wins (Ordered by Impact)

1. **Rewrite hero headlines first** — every demo needs an outcome-first headline from the positioning doc.
2. **Add audience signal** — one line per demo that says "Built for [persona]" (already defined in positioning doc).
3. **Remove tech jargon from hero** — "system," "platform," "multi-tenant," "rate limiting" should move to feature sections or footnotes.
4. **Use positioning pain-point hooks** — phrases like "from spreadsheet to spreadsheet," "where's that article?" and "who did what, when" are proven language from the positioning doc that isn't used yet.
